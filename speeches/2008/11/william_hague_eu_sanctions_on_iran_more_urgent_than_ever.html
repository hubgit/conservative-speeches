William Hague: EU sanctions on Iran more urgent than ever

Rt Hon William Hague MP, Tuesday, November 25 2008

"We do not have the luxury of waiting for the new US President to take office and launch an initiative on Iran that will somehow remove this difficult problem from our hands as some in Europe appear to be hoping.

By January, Iran will have accumulated a stockpile of nearly 1,000 kilograms of low-enriched uranium, the product of 6,000 centrifuge machines continuously churning out this material. When you consider that Iran had only a handful of these machines in 2006 and that it is under UN sanctions designed to make it stop this activity, it becomes clear how urgent the situation is.

1,000 kilograms of low enriched uranium is considered to be enough to produce at 20kgs of weapons-grade uranium, enough for one nuclear weapon. If Iran reaches that point the Middle East, already the epicentre of many of the world's gravest problems, will be irreversibly changed. We clearly do not have much time left.

It has long been my view, and the view of the Conservative Party, that if Iran is to be brought to the negotiating table, the EU will have to be willing to impose serious sanctions, and the United States of America will have to consider direct engagement with Iran. Whether a new administration does this and how it does it is a matter for America, although Britain can and should recommend ways in which the prospects of success can be enhanced.

A great deal of attention in the public debate focuses on what presumed concessions either side might have to make and what exactly should be put on the table, in search of a perfect formula which will guarantee success.

But we should not presume the content of an agreement when we have not even achieved the circumstances under which a return to negotiations is feasible. Firstly, any negotiations are likely to take place in secret, and we may not hear about them until after the event, as was the case with Henry Kissinger's secret mission to China in 1971. Secondly, the terms of the negotiations are likely to only become clear when all the parties involved come together.

What we should focus on is how we reverse the failures of the last two years which have brought us to the point where Iran is on the verge of a nuclear weapons capability. Negotiations with Iran need to take place from a position of strength, if it is to be dissuaded from its current path. This means that there has to be the very real prospect that if Iran does not accept talks, it will find itself frozen out of the international community, paying a serious economic price for its intransigence, and unable to access the investment and markets it needs if its own economic crisis is not to become paralysis.

Today, we are nowhere near that point. European countries have a particularly poor record:

There is still no formal Europe-wide ban on export credit guarantees that subsidise trade with Iran
There are still no restrictions whatsoever on European companies making new investments in Iranian oil and gas fields

Only 2 Iranian state-owned banks have been formally banned from operating in Europe, even though the UK recently submitted a list to the Security Council of 42 Iranian banks known to be directly and indirectly involved in dodging sanctions or transactions related to Iran's nuclear programme, 9 of which have offices in major European cities including Paris, Athens, Hamburg and London.

And there is still a swathe of Iranian companies involved in Iran's nuclear programme which the United States has targeted but which Europe has not.

There may be legitimate reasons why European countries have greater difficulty than the US in taking unilateral action because of a lack of domestic legislation, but there has been plenty of time for this to be rectified.

Overall, it is hard to escape the conclusion that some European countries lack the will to take action. Iran itself has drawn this conclusion - on 12th November Iran's Deputy Minister of Commerce boasted that 67% of Iran's $140 Billion foreign trade in 2007 came from Europe - Germany, France and Italy in particular - and that "EU members are not paying any attention to the UN resolutions against Iran."

Europe must wake up to the reality that weakness now only increases the risk of military confrontation, and that they cannot wish this problem away."
