Speech

A Welsh Conservative vision for a healthier Wales

Jonathan Morgan, Saturday, November 22 2008

"Boneddigion a boneddigesau, mae'n bleser mawr bod yn ôl yng Nghogledd Cymru unwaith eto. Ladies and Gentlemen, it is great to be back in North Wales.

It is a real pleasure to be here in Llandudno today; I think this is my third/fourth trip in as many months. It really is a special part of world. Why? Because it's turning blue!

We have had fantastic successes in North Wales over recent years. We have built on the strong foundations of our two North Wales Assembly Members - my colleagues Mark and Brynle - with David's victory in Clwyd West.

We followed this up last year with Darren's splendid victory over Government Minister, Alun Pugh. And this year at the local elections, we moved the party another step forward.

The most local councillors in Wales we have ever had - the biggest gains coming here in North Wales, in Denbighshire and Conwy. We have Cabinet posts in Denbighshire, Wrexham and Flintshire.

It's very clear for all to see...the resurgence of the Conservative Party in Wales is here in the North. We know the seats we've gained here, only paints half a picture.

The near misses we experienced last year in the likes of Delyn, Clwyd South and the Vale of Clwyd and in the local elections this year, show that we are poised for a very strong General Election campaign...and further successes.

During my recent visits, I have been excited by the enthusiasm and dedication of candidates we have for the General Election.

Guto Bebb, Antoinette Sandbach, Matt Wright, John Bell, Trefor Jones, Will Gallagher, Gareth Hughes. I know that we will convert all the hard work that all of you have put in.

The most important asset this Party has is not David Cameron...though he's not bad...he's not the most crucial part of our success. Nor is it us, the elected politicians. The most important asset it you - the members.

None of our successes have been possible without your support; your commitment; your effort.

We make these gains, these breakthroughs, not through warm rhetoric and prayers but through determination and sheer hard work.

From Jonathan in Europe and Cheryl in Westminster, right the way through the party machine, to you, the councillors in our communities and the activists on the streets.

We make these gains because we are a strong, united party - and that's what wins elections.

I know there can be a perception that due to the demography and topography of Wales, two separate elements of the Party exist in Wales; us in the North and them in the South.

Or where the Assembly is situated; us in the North and them in Cardiff!

We cannot afford such perceptions to become obstacles to our growth; barriers to our future success.

We need to engage with each other and work together because we are uniquely placed above all the parties in Wales.

We are not like Plaid who say one thing in the North and something completely different in the South...people have seen through their hypocrisy!

We are not like Labour who pin their hopes on the hinterland of the South Wales Valleys...that support is crumbling!

We are not like the Liberals who are thus far an irrelevance in Wales. We are a Party of principle; conviction; relevance; vision; of innovation. We do not follow the agenda - we lead the agenda. We do not say what we do - we do what we say;

We do not fight for particular interests or groups - we fight for everyone; for each community; for the good of each other and for the good of the UK as a whole.

Our appeal grows day-on-day because our values are shared by millions across the country; the values of strong family, strong community, fairness, trust, responsibility, choice, hard work and self-reliance.

As we embark on the European Election campaign let this be our mantra. Let the people of Wales and wider Britain know our values are as modern and relevant today as they have always been.

We are the Party of the people; for the people.

The only way to win the hearts and minds of Welsh voters is by committing ourselves as a Party, to greater engagement and collaboration with each other.

As many of you will be aware, I have already made a commitment to work closer with our Council Group Leaders and Health spokespeople to generate and develop policies for one of the most challenging areas of public policy that we face not just in Wales; but in the UK; our future health care needs.

As we go through yet another process of structural change in the Welsh NHS, probably the most significant and pertinent question that is on people's lips at the moment is...

'Who is in control of the NHS in Wales?'

The Assembly Government and the Health Minister in particular, are clear in their minds where power should lie - with politicians; and with herself.

As far as this Party is concerned, this is complete madness. We have had almost a decade of centralised control, targets, bureaucracy and political interference in the National Health Service. Therefore, the process of restructuring was needed - and was necessary.

However, the culmination of this process was to ensure that the NHS has a period of stability and direction, not further interference and change.

The main problem with our health system was that for decades the governance of the NHS and its strategic direction has been set by Ministerial whim - with no sense of long-term aims and objectives.

Therefore, in order for restructuring to work effectively, we had to remove party politicking and dogmatic posturing from the process.

We needed to take an innovative and systematic approach, which would ensure that the NHS in Wales was streamlined, clinically-led and was at arms-length from government; an approach capable of delivering first-class services free at the point of use in the 21st century.

The new Coalition Government had a real opportunity to deliver this approach.

To learn from past mistakes and set a new structure where politicians would be removed from the day-to-day running of our health service and power would be given back to clinicians - back to the professionals.

In August, I visited a number of hospitals and voluntary organisations across North Wales.

I went to Wrexham Maelor and Ysbyty Glan Clwyd.

I visited Llangollen Community Hospital with John Bell; Mold Community Hospital with Antoinette; and Denbigh's Disability Forum with Matt Wright and local Councillors John Bellis and Dianna Hannan.

I am sure they will agree with me that during those visits, the knowledge, expertise and dedication of the NHS staff was extensive and without question.

They knew their patients; they knew what resources were needed to do the job; and knew what they wanted from the Government in the way of future support and encouragement.

How can it be possible then, for Labour and Plaid to negate that depth and wealth of knowledge and understanding and dictate to them how their hospital should be run and what targets they should be fulfilling?  

We thought it was blindingly obvious that the only way for stability to be enshrined in the running of the NHS in the future, was for Ministerial interference to be reduced as far as possible.

That is why I called for an All Wales Advisory Board to be clinically led, ensuring that the NHS is driven by performance management and clinical outcomes, not political expediency or cheap opportunism.

I must be the only member of the Shadow Cabinet who is keen to give up most of his powers on becoming a minister!

But what has our Health Minister done?

Completely blinkered to the consultation process she undertook, she has politicised the Welsh NHS in a way which I would not have believed possible.

By making herself the Chair of this new National Advisory Board, Edwina Hart will be directly responsible for primary care, community care and mental health.

Let us be clear, what she has embarked on is a very dangerous exercise. What she has effectively done is to ensure that a politician is in charge of day to day decisions.

Not helping to set a broad strategic direction but signing off stationary orders. Not focusing on public health but sitting in endless committee meetings.

Not allowing clinicians and managers to take the lead but getting in their way at every opportunity.

We have gone from one extreme to the other - 22 Local Health Boards to a state controlled governing body, bordering on old Soviet style command and control.

The Minister says that she wants this restructuring process to be the last for a generation.

In my opinion, this could well be another disaster by the Labour Government that one day - and probably sooner rather than later - will have to be completely unpicked.

Again costing us time, a large amount of public money, jeopardising service delivery and severely denting the morale of our health service most valuable assets - its staff.

Let me assure you all here today and the wider Welsh public, as soon as we have the chance to bring about change at the next Assembly Elections, I as a future health minister, will be radical and ambitious about the future direction of out healthcare system.

The people of Wales will rightly ask us how we are different from Labour and Plaid Cymru. So let me tell you.

One of the first decisions I will make is that I will not be chair of the National Health Service.

There is no justification for such a position when there are so many clinical experts out there that could do the job far better than I could.

They are the people who know more about delivering a modern healthcare service than any politician could ever hope to know.

The second major decision I will make would be to ensure that we expand the role of the independent sector.

This Government has struggled to recognise the importance of the non-statutory sector and the way that they can be more fully engaged in planning and delivering services.

The NHS benefits from the capacity in the independent sector, whether the traditional private sector or voluntary charitable organisations who provide a range of services.

The planning of services from the non-statutory sector needs to be fully explored in the operation of the new NHS structures to ensure that patients continue to benefit.

I will ensure that there is stronger engagement with the non-statutory sector in the planning and delivery of services.

We need to be open and honest with the people of Wales about how our NHS is actually delivered.

In this country healthcare has always relied on the involvement of the non-statutory sector.

Labour and Plaid are conning the people of Wales into believing that the state can provide everything. It cannot.

The third major decision I will take, which affects not just the ability of the NHS to provide treatment for those who need it in the next 20-30 years, is the public health time bomb.

One of the greatest challenges we face within the healthcare agenda in Wales is the poor levels of public health.

Wales has some of the worst public health levels not just in Britain but in Europe.

Well over half of our adult population is obese; over 25% are regular smokers; 40% of adults drink over their recommended safe limits with nearly 30% drinking to excess or 'binge drink'.

Cardiff is seen as the UK's 'capital city of booze'.

But the most startling statistic for me is that if we continue on this present road, the current generation of children will die of before their parents because of poor lifestyle conditions.

This cannot be right and we cannot afford to let this happen.

I believe that the Assembly Government must introduce its own public health reforms to counter our poor lifestyle choices which, in 20-30 years, will not just see our nation more unhealthy than it is now but will put a considerable strain on the health service who is dealing with it.

The new Government of Wales Act has given Wales, has given us as politicians, a real opportunity to lead the way in developing the next generation of health improvement and protection legislation.

As a small nation, we can allow ourselves the flexibility to be innovative and pioneering in our legislation and how we deliver better public health services.

We must afford ourselves the chance to deliver new solutions to this problem.

Let me make this absolutely clear, as a Party that aspires to government, will be failing in our duty if we buried our heads in the sand.

I want us to be ambitious in delivering public health reform to help people lead healthier lives.

I visited the University of Swansea's Institute of Life Sciences yesterday.

I was amazed at the unique and world-class project that they are developing there.

They see the future of delivering the best healthcare globally as a multi-disciplinary exercise.

Where the best ideas and best practice are not compartmentalised but shared and nurtured across all sectors - academic knowledge; private enterprise and entrepreneurship and public delivery.

They are learning and working in new and dynamic ways to ensure that innovative ideas are taken from the lab bench to the patient bedside.

We should be using a similar model to pioneer our own policy ideas and legislative solutions to generate the tangible outcomes we all want to see; better health for all.

I certainly do not envisaged new public health legislation to be a collection of prohibitions but it would encompass a broad range of solutions to tackle problems like the lack of physical exercise in schools; the deficiencies in the planning system that doesn't provide enough open spaces; and the lack of incentives for local authorities to provide more leisure facilities; and the madness of our licensing laws that turns our town centres into havens of binge drinking.

We will need future government ministers to think outside of their departmental boxes.

No longer can government tackle the issue of public health without looking at those areas of health, education, environment, transport and planning.

It is not about telling people what they can and cannot do.

This is about government bringing in radical reform that will help people make better choices where they are unable to do so at the moment.

We cannot lecture people about the food they eat if they lack the knowledge and skills to prepare and cook a healthy meal.

We cannot lecture children about taking more exercise where exercise in our schools is not given priority and where the provision of open spaces takes second place in the planning of our towns and residential areas.

On the third anniversary of the Labour's relaxation of licensing laws, we cannot lecture people about the dangers of binge drinking where the licensing system has an inbuilt bias towards providing more pubs and nightclubs and 24 licences for cheap supermarket booze.

Nor can we lecture people about making better use of our trains and buses, cycle and walk routes if those opportunities simply do not exist.

As a Conservative, I believe passionate about an individuals right to chose but the ability to exercise that right is fashioned according to the options we have and the attitudes of society.

It would be intended to help fashion a society where people are encouraged to make healthier choices - such as making it easier to cycle or walk rather than drive.

Most public health legislation has been around infectious disease, but the much more difficult challenge is what do you do with all those things that determine the health of the people.

Long-term good health for Wales is the new prosperity we should aim for; long-term poor health is the new poverty we must avoid.

Our new legislation should be informed by Sweden's public health law which is based on the determinants of health.

The Swedish Public Health Act has the twin aims of improving public health in general and reducing differences in the health between various population groups.

It has 11 target areas including reducing the use of tobacco and alcohol, a drug-free society and a reduction in the harmful effects of excessive gambling, and good eating habits and safe food stuffs.

Yes some steps are being taken in Wales to address unhealthy lifestyles, the root causes of ill health and underlying health inequalities.

But there is no single measure which explicitly sets out these aims or enshrines them.

We need a range of initiatives in place to improve the situation, especially to encourage people from a young age to develop good healthy habits they can take into adulthood.

As a political party, in tackling the big picture we will show our own confidence in our vision for a healthier Wales and our determination to deliver not just better health services in the short term but a healthier society in the longer term."
